# Sudoku 6×6

This document brings together two technical records related to the development of a Sudoku 6×6 application with 2×3 subgrids.

The first part documents the initial development of the application. The second part documents a later exploration of board compression and compact encoding strategies, including the line of reasoning that eventually informed the algorithm implemented for v1.1.0.

Both parts are retrospective records written after reviewing the original chats.

This part records the initial development of the Sudoku 6×6 application.

The application was developed almost entirely by **Claude Sonnet 4.6 extended**, with only minimal intervention from the author, limited essentially to a small number of adjustments during development, including a change to the maximum value of one slider at a certain point in the process.

## Technical record of the development of a 6 × 6 Sudoku with 2 × 3 subgrids, generated in collaboration with Claude Sonnet 4.6

What follows is a third-person record of the development process of a 6 × 6 Sudoku game with 2 × 3 subgrids carried out by **Alejandro** in collaboration with **Claude Sonnet 4.6**.

To review the full conversation, follow this link: https://claude.ai/share/5b4aa846-e87c-4fef-98d4-7d69d72c66dc

## 1. Initial request

The conversation began with a specific request:

> Can you make a simple sudoku with html, css, javascript, with 2×3 cells in 3×3 groups?

Claude responded with an initial functional version of the 6×6 board, including validation, hint, checking, and restart features.

## 2. Restart and difficulty levels

After the first version, Alejandro proposed extending the functionality:

> It's perfect. I want pressing "restart" to generate a new different sudoku. I also want the difficulty level to be selectable with more or fewer numbers when restarting.

In response, Claude added a modal with three predefined levels when pressing *Restart*:

- **Easy**: 24 visible clues (more given numbers).
- **Medium**: 18 clues (intermediate level).
- **Hard**: 13 clues (proposed minimum challenge).

Each selection generated a new puzzle with a unique solution and displayed the active level on screen. To achieve this, a backtracking generator was added together with a uniqueness check through `countSolutions`.

## 3. Uniqueness guarantee and search for maximum difficulty

Alejandro wanted to confirm solvability and explore a maximum-difficulty mode:

> Does the algorithm used in the code ensure that each sudoku can be solved and has only one solution? If so, would it be very complex to include an additional difficulty option for the highest possible difficulty? I do not want you to program it immediately. I want you to think about the logic required to achieve it.

Claude explained that `countSolutions` stops the search as soon as it detects two solutions, thereby preserving uniqueness. He also clarified that fewer clues do not automatically imply greater human difficulty; the actual difficulty depends on the techniques required to solve the board.

The final recommendation was to approximate maximum difficulty by minimizing clues and discarding boards whose initial configuration allowed any cell to be solved with basic techniques. In practice, the filter relied on *naked singles* and *hidden singles*.

## 4. Implementation of the **Maximum** mode and difficulty metric

Alejandro then asked for a detailed description of the plan to implement maximum difficulty:

> Go ahead. First, tell me about your plan to make 6×6 sudokus with the highest possible difficulty.

Claude structured the plan in two layers: first minimize the number of clues, then filter out boards that still offered trivial moves at the start. A limit on the number of attempts was also introduced to avoid unbounded searches.

Alejandro proposed controlling the “depth of difficulty” and creating a mathematical difficulty quantifier. From that point, Claude derived a formula based on several puzzle metrics.

### Difficulty metrics and formula

Five metrics were defined to quantify difficulty:

| Metric | Brief meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **P — Given clues** | Number of pre-filled cells; fewer clues imply greater difficulty |
| **S — Direct moves** | Cells immediately solvable through *naked singles* or *hidden singles*; a low S indicates the absence of trivial moves |
| **D — Branching depth** | Number of trial-and-error levels required to solve the puzzle; a higher D means more branching |
| **C — Average candidates** | Average number of possible values per empty cell; greater ambiguity implies greater difficulty |
| **M — Minimum candidates** | Minimum number of candidates in any cell; a higher value indicates the absence of easy entries |

Each metric was normalized, weighted, and integrated into a theoretical total score of 1000 points. The formula used an exponential component for S and assigned the greatest weight to D. It was also noted that, in practice, the real ceiling would be lower than 1000.

## 5. Pareto dominance and interface refinements

While testing the maximum-difficulty mode, Alejandro proposed that the algorithm should not only search for the highest score, but compare puzzles based on the simultaneous improvement of all metrics. In his words:

> I changed the slider up to 1000 and it works well... I would like it, instead of searching for the maximum score of our formula... to search for the best algorithm according to the increase of each metric... each new puzzle found must be the result of matching the value of all metrics and surpassing at least one.

Claude recognized that this idea corresponded to **Pareto dominance** and adjusted the search so that a new puzzle would only be accepted if it worsened none of the metrics and improved at least one. The slider was later extended to 2000, then to 4000, and improvement indicators were added.

Additional interface changes were then introduced: removal of the numeric buttons, relocation of the search-status messages, and automatic checking when the board was completed.

## 6. Compact board encoding

Alejandro opened the most technical part of the conversation with the following prompt:

> What is the shortest code of letters and numbers that could be used to reconstruct a random initial position for the sudoku? Do not program it, let us just think about this problem.

From that point, Claude estimated the raw state space of the board as 7^36 states, equivalent to about 101 bits, and compared several encoding schemes. The initial conclusion was that a direct base-62 representation could reduce the full board to 17 characters.

Alejandro then explicitly formulated his own compression idea, which should be preserved because it guided the rest of the analysis:

> Explain a bit more the optimization you mention.  
> Then consider this initial analysis:  
> Think about one row.  
> The first cell has 7 states, the second 6, and so on until the sixth cell, which has 2 possible states.  
> In the second row we start with 6 possible states: 6,5,4,3,2  
> Third row: 5,5,5,4,3,2  
> fourth: 4,4,4,4,3,2  
> Etc.

That approach shifted the analysis from a direct board encoding toward a combinatorial estimate of valid states. Claude responded by splitting the problem into two parts: the map of given positions and the values of the given cells, and then developed the row-by-row model while also incorporating the 2×3 box constraints.

The result was a progressive conceptual compression:

- 101 bits for the raw board.
- ~75 bits by separating mask and given values.
- ~45 bits after incorporating stronger structural constraints.
- 25 bits to index a complete solution among the 28,200,960 valid solutions.
- ~31 bits to combinatorially encode which cells are revealed.

With that decomposition, Claude proposed combining the solution index and the combinatorial rank of the mask into a single integer, leading to a practical puzzle code of **10 characters** for different difficulty levels.

## 7. Integration of the encoding system

After designing the encoding scheme, Alejandro asked for it to be integrated into the interface:

> Go ahead. The idea is to place an input field to enter the code so that the sudoku starts from there... next to the input there should be a button to copy the reference code and one to load the code into the board.

Claude implemented this functionality. Each puzzle was converted into a base-7 representation, then into base 62, and displayed in a text field. **Copy** and **Load** actions were added, together with validations for length, alphabet, consistency with sudoku rules, existence of a solution, and uniqueness. The code updated automatically whenever the board changed.

## Conclusion

The development progressed from a basic 6×6 sudoku to a version with random puzzle generation, difficulty control, maximum mode, an evaluation formula, Pareto-dominance-based search, interface refinements, and a compact encoding system for sharing boards.

# Part II — Compression and encoding design

*Document generated by ChatGPT 5.4 after reviewing the original chat. Date: April 4, 2026.*

This part records a technical exploration centered on the compression of 6 × 6 Sudoku states.

The analysis was carried out through a conversation between **Alejandro** and **Gemini 3.1 Pro** on **April 4, 2026**. It progressively moved from generic matrix compression toward domain-specific representations constrained by Sudoku rules, and later informed the algorithmic direction implemented for **v1.1.0** in pair programming with **Claude Code via Cursor**.

## Technical record of a 6 × 6 Sudoku compression and encoding exploration, based on discussions with Gemini 3.1 Pro

What follows is a third-person record of a technical exploration on how to represent a **6 × 6 Sudoku with 2 × 3 subgrids** using the smallest possible number of characters.

The interaction moved from generic compression ideas toward increasingly specialized methods that attempted to exploit the combinatorial structure of the puzzle itself.

## 1. From general matrix compression to the Sudoku domain

The conversation began with a broad question about the compression of **6 x 6 numeric matrices**. Gemini first processed the problem through a broad technical taxonomy rather than through a single candidate solution. Among the lossless ideas it mentioned were **ZIP / LZ77**, **Huffman coding**, **Run Length Encoding (RLE)**, **Compressed Sparse Row (CSR)**, **delta encoding**, and **bit-packing**. For lossy or approximation-oriented settings it also mentioned **DCT**, **SVD**, and **autoencoders**. Most of these did not remain relevant to the Sudoku itself, but they formed the initial analytical frame through which Gemini classified the problem before the domain was narrowed.

The direction changed once Alejandro specified the real target: a **6 x 6 Sudoku whose cells take values from 0 to 6**. He also suggested that repeated zeros might make RLE useful. From there, the problem stopped being one of general-purpose compression and became a problem of **state encoding under constraints**, where the alphabet is tiny and the board size is fixed. Gemini then proposed three immediate Sudoku-specific approaches: **bit-packing**, an **RLE scheme with a reserved marker**, and representing the whole board as a single large **base-7** number.

## 2. The objective shifts from bytes to characters

A second important turn occurred when Alejandro clarified that his real objective was not simply low-level storage efficiency, but a **code with the smallest possible number of visible characters** representing the board. That changed the optimization target from bytes and machine operations to textual compactness.

Gemini responded by suggesting two immediate textual encodings. One grouped cells in pairs and mapped each pair to a printable character, yielding an **18-character** representation. The other took a more extreme route, closer to a **code-golf style Unicode packing**, by mapping one full row into one visible Unicode symbol and producing a **6-character** encoding at the cost of readability and practical portability. This established one of the main tensions in the exploration: the shortest representation in character count is not always the most usable one for human handling or robust transmission.

## 3. First structural compression idea based on decreasing choices

Alejandro then proposed the first major domain-based idea of the conversation: instead of treating the board as a flat array of 36 independent positions, he tried to count how the number of available choices decreases across rows. His reasoning was that the first row could be understood through a descending multiplicative pattern, and that lower rows would continue that logic with progressively smaller choice spaces.

Gemini recognized the core intuition as a valid move toward **compression based on the rules of the system** rather than on raw symbol storage. However, it also pointed out the key obstacle: the presence of **0 as an empty-cell value** breaks the clean factorial behavior, because zeros may repeat while digits 1 through 6 may not. That meant the row-based multiplicative idea worked much better for completed valid placements than for arbitrary partial boards with blanks. In response, Gemini temporarily fell back to a more neutral baseline: treating the board as a base-7 object, which led to the estimate of **17 Base64 characters** for the unconstrained full 6 x 6 state space.

At this stage Gemini also noted that, if one insisted on counting only legal continuations at every step, the problem would eventually require some form of **backtracking** or recursive future counting, because partial Sudoku states do not admit a simple closed multiplicative formula once row, column, and box interactions are all active.

## 4. The 2 x 3 abstraction and factorial-style indexing

The conversation then passed through a smaller abstraction: first binary matrices, and then a **2 x 3 matrix**. Gemini explained that a 2 x 3 matrix of zeros and ones contains exactly 6 bits of information, and can therefore be stored as a single byte or represented by one Base64 character. That simpler setting became a stepping stone toward more structured encodings.

Alejandro then reframed the problem again: suppose one symbol already encodes which positions in the 2 x 3 block are occupied, while the occupied cells contain **non-repeated digits from 1 to 6**. Gemini’s answer was to split the block into two parts: a **mask** for occupied positions and an encoding of the ordered non-repeated digits. For the second part, Gemini introduced a factorial-style ranking process based on a shrinking candidate list, effectively describing a **Lehmer-like indexing method** and explicitly relating it to the **factorial number system**. In the fully occupied case, this yielded **6! = 720** possible arrangements, meaning the payload could be represented by an integer from 0 to 719.

That part of the dialogue was important because it introduced a more powerful principle: instead of storing the symbols directly, one can store the **index of a legal arrangement within its state space**. Compression thus becomes a question of counting valid states and ranking them.

## 5. Integrated state counting versus separate fields

Alejandro then noticed an inconsistency in the earlier treatment of mask and payload as separate pieces. That observation forced a refinement. Gemini acknowledged that allocating a worst-case budget for the numeric payload wastes space when only a few cells are occupied, because many of the reserved states are never needed.

The reply therefore moved toward a full **integrated state count** for the 2 x 3 case. Gemini summed the number of legal configurations for each possible count of occupied cells and arrived at a total of **13,327** valid states. From there, it concluded that the whole 2 x 3 configuration could be represented as a single integer in the range 0 to 13,326, requiring only **14 bits**, and therefore fitting into **3 letters** if a 26-symbol alphabet were used. This clarified a crucial idea that remained relevant for the Sudoku itself: the true entropy lies in the total legal configuration space, not in a naive concatenation of independent fields.

## 6. Returning to the full Sudoku: 17 Base64 characters as the naive bound

When the discussion returned to the full 6 x 6 Sudoku, Alejandro asked for direct answers about the size of different representations. Gemini stated that **six distinct digits from 1 to 6** can be encoded in **2 Base64 characters**, because 2 Base64 symbols cover 4096 states, well above the 720 permutations of six distinct digits.

Gemini also summarized the naive full-board result: if all 36 cells are treated as independent positions with 7 possible values each, then the board spans **7^36** states, requires **102 bits**, and therefore occupies **17 Base64 characters**. That became the reference point from which every later improvement was measured.

Gemini also briefly proposed an alternative direct serialization path at this stage: take the full board as a large **base-7 integer**, then serialize that integer in **Base85** instead of Base64. Under that approach, the board still occupies the same raw information content, but the denser output alphabet reduces the visible string to **17 Base85 characters** for the unconstrained board. That early Base85 idea did not yet exploit Sudoku legality, but it foreshadowed the implementation path that would later matter most.

## 7. Alternative alphabets and the non-advantage of 2D storage

Alejandro then asked whether other bases using more recognizable symbols could reduce the character count further. Gemini compared **Base64** with denser alphabets such as **Base85**, **Base94**, emoji-based schemes such as **Base1024** or **Base2048**, and larger Unicode-based alphabets such as **Base4096**. The essential trade-off was clear: higher-base alphabets can reduce visible length, but usually at the cost of portability, simplicity, and ease of human handling.

Gemini also distinguished between textual alphabets and physical or optical encodings. It mentioned that a two-dimensional physical representation such as **Micro QR**, **Aztec**, or similar matrix codes can improve physical density and scanning convenience, but it does **not** increase the underlying information capacity of the encoded state.

A related question followed: could a two-dimensional representation store more information than a linear string? Gemini answered no in strict information-theoretic terms. A 6 x 6 matrix and a sequence of 36 positions have the same storage capacity if each position has the same alphabet. However, it noted that a 2D view can still be advantageous for **processing** the constraints of the puzzle or for **physical layout**, such as QR-like encodings. This helped separate the idea of storage capacity from the idea of representation geometry.

## 8. From 17 characters to an 11-character theoretical limit

The next decisive step occurred when Alejandro asked whether those 17 Base64 characters could be compressed further without information loss. Gemini answered that arbitrary 17-character Base64 strings cannot be losslessly compressed in general, invoking the **pigeonhole principle** and explicitly relating the binary case to **Shannon-style information-theoretic entropy**. But Sudoku boards are not arbitrary strings: they belong to a highly constrained state space.

Gemini then proposed a theoretical upper bound based on two ingredients: the number of completed valid **6 x 6 Sudokus with 2 x 3 blocks**, given as **28,200,960**, and the **2^36** possible visibility masks for the cells. Multiplying both yielded a total state count of about **1.93 x 10^18**, which requires **61 bits**, and therefore **11 Base64 characters**. In that formulation, the unconstrained 17-character model gave way to a much tighter bound driven by Sudoku legality.

## 9. The dynamic-base algorithm

When Alejandro asked how such an algorithm might be built, Gemini distinguished between the absolute optimum and what is computationally practical. It explained that truly reaching the 11-character lower bound would require knowing, at each step, how many valid full continuations remain from that point onward, which leads to heavy combinatorial counting and effectively to repeated **backtracking** over future legal branches.

As a more workable alternative, Gemini proposed what it described as a **dynamic-base** or **forward-constraint-tracking** approach. The core idea was to traverse the board cell by cell while maintaining trackers for rows, columns, and blocks. For each cell, the encoder would construct the local list of legal options, determine the index of the actual value inside that local list, and fold that index into a growing accumulator whose base changes dynamically according to the number of legal choices at that moment. Later, when Alejandro asked for a purely conceptual explanation, Gemini reformulated the same method in abstract terms using an **accumulator**, a **multiplier**, and **logical trackers**.

This algorithm was presented not as the theoretical optimum, but as a strong compromise: it would avoid wasting capacity on illegal choices and would likely reduce the naive 17-character encoding to something closer to **12 or 13 characters**, while remaining far easier to implement than a globally optimal ranking over all legal futures.

## 10. A new axis of traversal: encoding by number and by box

The final and most structurally interesting part of the conversation came from Alejandro’s own alternative view of the completed Sudoku. Instead of traversing the board cell by cell, he proposed traversing it **number by number across the six 2 x 3 groups**. For each digit, he estimated how many placements remain possible in each successive group, producing the sequences:

- 1: 6, 3, 4, 2, 2, 1
- 2: 5, 3, 4, 2, 2, 1
- 3: 4, 3, 4, 2, 2, 1
- 4: 3, 3, 3, 2, 2, 1
- 5: 2, 2, 2, 1, 1, 1
- 6: 1, 1, 1, 1, 1, 1

Gemini explicitly recognized this as close in spirit to **Algorithm X**, **Donald Knuth**, and **exact cover** reasoning, because it changed the axis of traversal from locations to entities. Although Gemini first objected that the multiplicative pattern could not remain completely fixed under all geometric interactions, Alejandro refined the claim and reframed it as a **worst-case static upper bound** rather than an exact dynamic count. Gemini then accepted that corrected interpretation. Multiplying those worst-case factors yielded **11,466,178,560** possible resolved-board states under Alejandro’s model, which fit into **34 bits**, that is, **6 Base64 characters**.

## 11. Resolved board plus zero mask

From there, Alejandro proposed a practical architecture: encode a **resolved board** with his worst-case multiplicative model, and then attach a separate mask indicating where the visible numbers and zeros are. Gemini compared this with the earlier theoretical optimum. Under that scheme, the solved board would cost **34 bits**, while the full visibility mask would cost **36 bits**, giving a total of **70 bits**, or **12 Base64 characters**.

That was only one character worse than the **11-character** theoretical optimum, but dramatically easier to reason about and implement. Gemini explicitly framed it as a classic engineering compromise: the theoretical 11-character design saves a few bits but requires much heavier constraint reasoning, whereas the 12-character design sacrifices only one character while remaining simple, direct, and computationally light.

By this point the conversation had already passed through a long chain of algorithmic and mathematical references: **LZ77**, **Huffman**, **RLE**, **CSR**, **delta encoding**, **bit-packing**, **DCT**, **SVD**, **autoencoders**, **Lehmer-style ranking**, the **factorial number system**, **backtracking**, **dynamic-base encoding**, **Algorithm X**, **exact cover**, the **pigeonhole principle**, and **Shannon-style entropy**. Not all of them remained central to the final design, but they form part of the technical record of how Gemini processed the search space before the architecture settled.

## 12. Base85 as the practical implementation path

A final practical shift occurred when Alejandro asked what would happen if the resulting code were represented not in Base64 but in **Base85**. Gemini described that as the decisive move for squeezing more density out of plain text without moving into fragile Unicode-heavy schemes. It specifically highlighted **Ascii85** and then recommended **Z85** as a safer modern alphabet for software use, because Z85 was designed to behave more predictably in source code, URLs, and structured text.

Under this view, Alejandro’s practical **70-bit** design no longer required **12 Base64 characters**. Using **Base85**, the same information could be carried in **11 characters**, because 10 Base85 characters are slightly insufficient for the full 70-bit range, while 11 are enough. This meant that the pragmatic worst-case static model, once paired with a denser output alphabet, reached the same visible length as the earlier **11-character Base64 theoretical limit**, but without requiring the heavier dynamic counting logic.

This point matters historically in the record because it is where the design stopped being merely comparative and became implementation-relevant. The algorithm that was ultimately implemented for **v1.1.0** of the application, in pair programming with **Claude Code via Cursor**, followed this practical route: a structurally simpler board model combined with a denser textual alphabet, rather than the globally optimal but computationally heavier legal-state ranking.

## 13. The unresolved problem of the zero mask

The conversation closed on the question of whether the **mask of zeros** could itself be improved. Gemini noted that this part is expensive precisely because, unlike the solved board, the visibility pattern has very weak structure if left fully unconstrained. A free 36-cell binary mask carries **36 bits** of raw entropy and therefore costs **6 Base64 characters** by itself.

Several possible reductions were discussed, but only by imposing extra rules. If the visible clues are forced to have **rotational symmetry**, the mask can drop from 36 bits to **18 bits**, yielding a total of **9 Base64 characters** when combined with the 34-bit solved-board model. If instead the puzzle generator always uses a fixed number of visible clues, the mask can be encoded combinatorially and may also shrink. Alejandro’s own final intuition was to store the minority class, choosing between zeros and occupied cells depending on which are fewer, but Gemini argued from an information-theoretic standpoint that in the worst case the metadata needed to describe that choice can erase the gain. That left the compression of the clue mask as the main open design question.

## Conclusion

The exploration moved through several conceptual layers. It began with generic matrix compression, narrowed to the 6 x 6 Sudoku as a finite state space, detoured through binary and 2 x 3 abstractions, and gradually shifted from naive symbol packing toward **ranking legal states under combinatorial constraints**.

The most important progression was this:

- from **raw board encoding** to **constraint-aware encoding**,
- from **flat storage of values** to **enumeration of legal states**,
- from **cell-by-cell traversal** to **dynamic local bases**,
- toward a more structural approach based on **placing each number across the six boxes of the completed board**, and finally
- toward a practical serialization decision in which **Base85**, especially in its **Z85-style** framing, became the preferred output layer for the implemented version.

The conversation did not end with a single definitive perfect encoder. Instead, it clarified the trade-off landscape. The naive unconstrained model requires **17 Base64 characters**. A legality-aware theoretical limit reduces that to **11**. Alejandro’s static worst-case model for resolved boards plus a clue mask reaches **12 Base64 characters**, while remaining much simpler to implement. Once that same 70-bit design is serialized through **Base85**, it also reaches **11 visible characters**, which is why the output alphabet became a decisive engineering choice rather than a cosmetic detail.

The unresolved frontier is therefore not only the solved board itself, but also the efficient representation of the visibility mask under useful design constraints. The implementation that followed chose not the mathematically purest encoder, but the most balanced one: simple enough to implement cleanly, dense enough to be practically elegant, and well aligned with the final Base85-oriented text representation.

The implementation of the algorithm was carried out for **v1.1.0** of the application, in pair programming with **Claude Code via Cursor**.
